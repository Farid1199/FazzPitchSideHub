What is Non-League Football?

“Non-league” is the collective term for the leagues and clubs below the English Football League (EFL). It encompasses everything from the National League (Step 1) all the way down to weekend park football. There are over 40,000 clubs and hundreds of leagues across England.

Non-league is the heartbeat of English football. It’s where future professionals are discovered, where communities come together, and where anyone — regardless of background — can get involved in the game through playing, coaching, scouting or volunteering.

The English Football Pyramid

The pyramid is the interconnected league structure that links grassroots football all the way up to the Premier League. Clubs can rise and fall through promotion and relegation.

Semi-Professional vs Amateur

Semi-Professional

  • Players receive a wage or match fee
  • Training 2–4 evenings per week
  • Most players have a day job alongside
  • Typically Steps 1–4 (Tiers 5–8)
  • Contracts & registration rules apply

Amateur

  • No payment — play purely for the love of it
  • Training 1–2 evenings per week (varies)
  • Open to all skill levels
  • Typically Steps 5–7 (Tiers 9+)
  • Less formal registration requirements

Note: Some Step 1 (National League) clubs are now fully professional, paying full-time wages. The lines between “non-league” and “league” are increasingly blurred at the top end.

How Promotion & Relegation Works

The beauty of the English system is that any club can theoretically rise from a park pitch to the Premier League. Each season:

Promotion

The top teams in each league go up. Often the champion goes up automatically, with a play-off for additional places. At lower steps, multiple teams can be promoted.

Relegation

Bottom teams drop down to the league below. This applies all the way from the Premier League to lower non-league, though exact rules vary by league.

Ground Requirements

Many non-league clubs miss out on promotion because they can’t meet ground grading requirements (floodlights, capacity, covered seating). This is one of the biggest challenges in non-league.

Step-by-Step Guide

Step 1 National League

The highest non-league division. 24 clubs compete. The champion is promoted to League Two (EFL). Up to 3 additional clubs go up via play-offs. Many clubs here operate full-time. Average gates: 2,000–8,000. Notable examples: Wrexham (before EFL return), Stockport County, Notts County.

Step 2 National League North & South

Two parallel divisions of 24 clubs each covering the north and south of England. Champions promoted directly; play-off winner also goes up. Mix of full-time and semi-pro clubs. Average gates: 500–3,000.

Step 3 Regional Premier Divisions

Three leagues: Northern Premier League Premier, Southern Premier League Premier (South & Central), and Isthmian League Premier. Mostly semi-professional. This is the highest level where most “non-league fans” engage regularly. Gates: 200–1,500.

Step 4 Regional Division One

Six divisions feeding into Step 3. Mix of paid and unpaid players. Many clubs operate on shoestring budgets. Players often combine football with full-time work. This is where many promising talents are spotted by scouts. Gates: 50–500.

Steps 5–6 Feeder Leagues

Dozens of regional leagues including the Combined Counties, Midland Football League, Eastern Counties, and more. Predominantly amateur. Some offer small expenses. This is the entry point for ambitious clubs and players looking to climb the pyramid.

Step 7 County & Grassroots

County leagues and Sunday leagues. Everyone is welcome — no trials needed. This is where most people first experience organised football. Clubs are community-run and always looking for new players, managers, and volunteers.

Get Involved in Non-League

Play

Find a club at your level and start playing. No experience required at grassroots level.

Coach / Manage

Get your coaching badges and lead a team. Clubs are always looking for new managers.

Scout

Develop your eye for talent and help clubs discover their next star signing.
