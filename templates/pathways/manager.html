Overview

Managing a football team is one of the most rewarding roles in the sport. At non-league level, managers are responsible for coaching sessions, recruitment, tactics, match-day management, and often much more behind the scenes.

You do not need to have been a professional player to become a successful manager. Many of the best non-league managers came through grassroots coaching, building their experience from local Sunday league teams up through the pyramid.

Coaching Career Path

1

Start Volunteering

Help out at a local youth team or Sunday league side. Get hands-on experience running sessions and organising match days.

2

Get Your First Coaching Badge

The FA Introduction to Coaching (formerly Level 1) is the entry point. Costs around £150 and takes a weekend.

3

Coach a Team

Take charge of a youth or adult team. Sunday league or local county league clubs often need coaches and assistant managers.

4

Progress Your Qualifications

Move through UEFA C, UEFA B and beyond. Higher badges open doors to Step 3+ management roles.

5

Become an Assistant Manager

Work alongside an experienced manager at Step 4–6. Learn the tactical and man-management side of the role.

6

Manage Your Own Team

Take full charge of a non-league side. Build a squad, set tactics, and compete in the pyramid.

Coaching Qualifications

The FA runs a structured coaching pathway. Here are the key levels:

L1

FA Introduction to Coaching

Entry-level badge. Covers session planning, basic skills development. Weekend course (~£150).

L2

FA Level 2 / UEFA C Licence

Develops ability to plan and deliver sessions. Required to manage most Step 5+ teams (~£300).

L3

UEFA B Licence

Advanced coaching badge. Covers in-depth tactical and technical coaching. Year-long programme (~£1,000).

L4

UEFA A Licence

Elite-level coaching qualification. Required for National League management and above.

Manager Responsibilities

At non-league level, a manager often wears many hats. Here’s what the role typically involves:

Training Sessions

Planning and delivering weekly sessions to develop player skills and tactical understanding.

Tactics & Selection

Choosing formations, match-day squads, substitutions and set-piece routines.

Recruitment

Scouting and signing players. Running trials and building a competitive squad.

Man Management

Motivation, discipline, dealing with player egos, and building team culture.

Getting Experience

The best way to learn management is by doing it. Here are practical ways to build experience:

  • Volunteer as an assistant at a local Sunday league or youth club.
  • Offer to run training sessions — even at a pub team, real sessions build real experience.
  • Network at non-league matches — attend local games and talk to managers and chairmen.
  • Apply for vacant roles — Step 6–7 clubs frequently advertise for managers on social media and County FA boards.
  • Build your profile on FAZZ PitchsideHub — verified managers can post opportunities, manage their club, and attract players.
