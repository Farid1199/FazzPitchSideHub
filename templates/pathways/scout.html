Overview

Football scouts are the eyes and ears of clubs. They identify talented players, assess their abilities, and recommend signings. At non-league level, scouting is vital — clubs rely on scouts to find players who can make a difference on a limited budget.

Scouting is one of the most accessible roles in football. There is no age limit, you don’t need to have been a player, and there are now formal qualifications that give you credibility. If you have a genuine eye for talent and a passion for watching football, this could be your pathway.

What Scouts Actually Do

Match Attendance

Watch live matches at all levels to assess players in competitive environments. Non-league games are ideal — affordable entry and raw talent on show.

Player Reports

Write detailed assessments of players covering technical ability, physical attributes, mentality, and potential for development.

Talent Identification

Spot the players that others miss. Identify hidden qualities such as decision-making, work rate and positional intelligence.

Recommendations

Report findings to club managers, directors of football, or recruitment teams. Your recommendation can change a player’s career.

Scouting Qualifications

While scouting doesn’t strictly require qualifications, having them significantly boosts your credibility with clubs:

FA Talent Identification Course

The primary scouting qualification from the FA. Teaches a structured approach to assessing players. Often delivered as a weekend course.

Advanced Talent ID

For experienced scouts looking to work at higher levels. Covers data analysis, video scouting, and advanced assessment frameworks.

Safeguarding Certificate

Required if you work with youth players. An FA Safeguarding course and an up-to-date DBS check are standard requirements.

Talent Identification — What to Look For

Good scouts don’t just watch the ball. They watch the players who don’t have it. Here are the four pillars of talent identification used by professional scouting networks:

Technical

First touch, passing range, dribbling ability, shooting technique. Can the player execute skills consistently under pressure?

Tactical

Positioning, movement off the ball, game reading, decision-making. Does the player understand their role in the team?

Physical

Speed, strength, stamina, agility, balance. Physical profiles vary by position — a striker’s needs differ from a full-back’s.

Psychological

Attitude, resilience, leadership, coachability. Often the most important factor — talent without mentality rarely succeeds.

Example Scouting Report Structure

A professional scouting report should follow a consistent template. Here’s a structure used at non-league and academy level:

  1. 1.Player Details — Name, age, position, current club, match observed.
  2. 2.Technical Assessment — Rate and comment on core technical skills.
  3. 3.Tactical Awareness — How the player reads the game and makes decisions.
  4. 4.Physical Profile — Speed, stamina, aerial ability, build.
  5. 5.Psychological Traits — Leadership, reaction to adversity, communication.
  6. 6.Key Strengths — Top 2–3 standout qualities.
  7. 7.Areas for Improvement — Honest assessment of weaknesses.
  8. 8.Overall Rating & Recommendation — Score out of 10 and whether to sign, monitor, or pass.
